Burgers

The other reliable kid win. Two of these you're already walking past — the rest are worth the trip.

Dick's Drive-In

25 min

The one locals name first, and it's not really about the burger — it's a 60-year-old walk-up window with no indoor seating, thin patties, and prices that feel like a typo. Order the Dick's Deluxe (two patties, cheese, lettuce, mayo, relish) and eat it on the hood of the car like everyone else. Pure Seattle nostalgia, and the kids get a milkshake for pocket change. $

Li'l Woody's

20 min

Burger-only menu, grass-fed beef from Royal Ranch Farm, toppings sourced regionally. Rotating specials board alongside the mainstays. If Dick's is nostalgia, this is the one where the burger is actually the point. $

Great State Burger

20 min

Classic burger, fries, shakes, done cleanly — from restaurateur Josh Henderson's Huxley Wallace Collective. Seven locations around the city, so check which is closest to wherever you've wandered. Fastest "everybody just eat something" option downtown. $

Katsu Burger

36 min

Japanese-inspired and genuinely different: panko-breaded, deep-fried patty (beef, pork, chicken, or tofu) with shredded cabbage, Japanese mayo, and tonkatsu sauce. The novelty sells it to kids who are bored of regular burgers by day three. $

Metropolitan Grill

22 min

A proper old-school steakhouse at 820 2nd Ave that happens to make a serious burger — thick mesquite-grilled patty, caramelized onions, cheddar and Swiss, thousand island. The dressed-up version of this list, and the bar is the easy way in without a steakhouse bill. $$$

Inside the Market: The Stuff Tourists Miss new section

Everything here is inside or under the market itself. Mostly free, mostly weird, all kid-bait.

The Rooftop Garden

Opened 2013 and still somehow quiet even when the arcades are shoulder-to-shoulder. Benches, a chalk wall the kids can draw on, and a big view. This is your reset button when everyone's overstimulated.

The Giant Shoe Museum

Bring quarters. You peek through stereoscope lenses at enormous shoes. It costs almost nothing, takes four minutes, and the kids will talk about it more than the Space Needle. Just outside Old Seattle Paperworks.

Market Magic Shop

Longest-running magic shop in the Pacific Northwest (founded 1973), third-oldest in the US. Vintage fortune-telling machines inside; look above the door for the magician poster whose eyes follow you.

Gem Heaven

Wall-to-wall minerals, crystals, and fossils. If you have a kid who picks up rocks, budget twenty minutes and ten dollars.

The Miniature Car Dealer

Exactly what it says — metal replica cars, planes, and trucks. Small, cheap, easy souvenir that isn't a t-shirt.

Golden Age Collectables

One of the oldest comic shops in the country, tucked in the Down Under levels. Comics, toys, and a lot of nostalgia depending on who's browsing.

The Down Under levels

Three floors below the main arcade, reached by stairs most people never take. Magic shop, comics, oddball vintage stores, and a fraction of the crowd.

Rachel the Piggy Bank + the fish throwers

The bronze pig under the clock is the classic photo; the coins actually fund market social services. Pike Place Fish Market throws salmon right behind her — stand and watch a few rounds, it's free.

The Gum Wall

Down in Post Alley below the market. Objectively disgusting, universally beloved by children. Bring a piece of gum so they can contribute.

Original Starbucks (1912 Pike Pl)

Worth knowing: the line is long, the coffee is normal, and it's not technically the first location — it moved here in 1976. Walk past it, take the photo of the sign, keep going.

The New Waterfront new section

The whole waterfront was rebuilt and a lot of this is brand new for 2026. It's entirely flat — bring the stroller even if nobody rides in it, it holds the water bottles.

Seattle Aquarium + Ocean Pavilion

16 min

Newly expanded pavilion — octopus, otters, touch tanks. 4.3★ (23,388)

The jellyfish climbing tower new

A 25-foot jellyfish you climb, with a slide, right outside the Aquarium's front doors. Free, no ticket needed. Do not walk past this expecting to leave in five minutes.

Pier 58 underwater-themed playground new

Opening summer 2026 — should be live for a July trip, but worth a quick check before you promise it.

Overlook Walk

15 min

Part bridge, part park — it connects Pike Place straight down to the Aquarium over Alaskan Way, with play elements, a café, and native plantings chosen by Indigenous landscape architect Valerie Segrest. This is now the best way to get from your hotel to the water. 4.6★

Pier 62 new

Giant chess, ping pong, a pop-up coffee shop, and free programming nearly every day in summer — live music, performances, food vendors. Check the day's schedule at waterfrontparkseattle.org before you go.

Miner's Landing / Pier 57

19 min

Seattle Great Wheel over the bay, plus an arcade and carousel — and a new Sasquatch Mountain virtual ride for 2026. 4.5★

Day sail from Pier 55 new

Sailing Seattle runs day and sunset sails out of Pier 55, and there's a tall ship at Bell Harbor Marina. 2026 season is running. Book ahead — small boats, they fill.

Ye Olde Curiosity Shop

Near Pier 54 — shrunken heads, mummies, taxidermy. Free to browse, delightfully weird.

Bainbridge Island Ferry

Walk-on round trip from the terminal (~10 min from the market), ~35 min each way. Cheap, scenic, no car. Ride out, get ice cream in Winslow, ride back — kills a whole afternoon happily.

Olympic Sculpture Park

Free, outdoor, ~15–20 min walk north along the water. Big open-air art kids can run around, plus Puget Sound and mountain views.

Other Things to See & Do Nearby

Beyond the Space Needle and the glass museum.

Pioneer Square Underground Tour

Walk through the literal buried streets of old Seattle from before the 1889 fire. Fun for kids 9+; check current minimum-age guidance.

Gates Foundation Discovery Center

Free, interactive global-health exhibits by Seattle Center. Overlooked because it isn't flashy.

Amazon Spheres

Free public visiting hours on weekends (schedule changes — check before going). Glass biodomes full of rainforest, downtown.

Klondike Gold Rush Museum new

Free national park site in Pioneer Square about the 1897 gold rush that built Seattle. Small, air-conditioned, and there's a junior ranger badge if that's a currency in your family.

Seattle Center Monorail new

Built for the 1962 World's Fair, still running. Two-minute ride from Westlake (5 min walk from the market) to the Space Needle. It's transportation and the activity.

Seattle Public Library, Central branch new

Rem Koolhaas's glass-and-steel building — free, indoors, and genuinely one of the best pieces of architecture in the country. The neon-yellow escalator and the spiral book ramp are the draws. Great rainy-hour move.

Smith Tower observatory new

1914 tower with a Prohibition-era bar at the top and an open-air deck. Cheaper and far less crowded than the Space Needle, with a better view of the Space Needle.

Argosy Harbor Cruise new

One-hour narrated loop of Elliott Bay from Pier 55. Low-effort, high-payoff on a clear July day — skyline, shipping cranes, and Rainier if the weather cooperates.

Seattle Great Wheel at dusk new

Worth timing deliberately. In July the sun doesn't set until ~9pm — go around 8:30 and you get the light show plus a sunset over the Olympics in one ride.

MarketFront (lower levels)

Most tourists never go down here — quieter, great water views, more local vendors, and it connects to the Overlook Walk.

Local Hidden Gems (Short Ride Out)

Worth a bus or rideshare — what locals actually recommend.

Ballard Locks fish ladder

Free, ~20 min out. In July you watch salmon migrate through underwater viewing windows and boats get raised and lowered. Probably the best free kid activity in the city, and July is exactly the right month.

Fremont Troll + Sunday Market

Giant troll crushing a real VW Beetle under the Aurora Bridge. Quirky neighborhood, rocket ship bolted to a building, good street food at the Sunday market.

Gas Works Park

Decommissioned gasification plant turned park in Wallingford — kite hill, skyline views across Lake Union. Locals' picnic spot.

Kerry Park

The postcard Space Needle/skyline shot, on Queen Anne hill. It's a small neighborhood park — five minutes and a photo, not an afternoon.

Golden Gardens Park

Real sandy beach with fire pits and mountain views, ~20 min drive. Combine with the Locks and Ballard dinner for a full day.

Discovery Park new

Seattle's largest park — 500 acres of bluff trails down to a lighthouse and a driftwood beach. The closest thing to real wilderness inside city limits. Loop trail is ~2.8 miles if the kids will walk it.

Alki Beach new

Take the water taxi from Pier 50 — that's the whole appeal, it's a boat ride to a beach with a skyline view back at the city. Beach volleyball, ice cream, sand. Very local, very easy.

Chihuly Garden and Glass new

You said you'd already covered the glass museum, but the note that matters: go in the evening. The outdoor garden lit at dusk is a completely different experience than the midday one everybody does.

Pacific Science Center new

At Seattle Center — tropical butterfly house, IMAX, hands-on physics floor. The rainy-day insurance policy if July decides to be Seattle about it.

Volunteer Park Conservatory new

Victorian glasshouse on Capitol Hill, cheap admission, plus a water tower next door you can climb for a free view. Nobody visiting Seattle does this.

My Picks

Cafe Campagne for breakfast · Pike Place Chowder for lunch · Alibi Room for pizza night · Old Stove Brewing for kid-friendly dinner-with-beer · and don't skip the Ballard Locks — best "wish we knew about this" spot for families visiting when the salmon are running.

From this round: Oriental Mart is the one I'd fight for — James Beard–recognized Filipino food from a grocery-stall counter, about a 15-minute walk from the Staypineapple, and almost nobody in that market knows it's there. Then the Rooftop Garden as your reset spot, the jellyfish tower on the way to the Aquarium, and the Great Wheel at 8:30pm for the sunset.

Worth knowing before you go

  • The market empties out by late afternoon. Most stalls close around 4–5pm even though restaurants stay open. Do your stall grazing before 3.
  • Mornings before 10am are a different market. Same fish throwers, a third of the people.
  • July sunset is ~9pm. Your evenings are much longer than they feel. Plan a real activity after dinner.
  • The Pink Door needs a reservation well ahead. Everything else on this list you can walk into.
  • Overlook Walk changed the geography. Old guides tell you to take the Pike Street Hillclimb stairs down to the waterfront — you don't have to anymore.
